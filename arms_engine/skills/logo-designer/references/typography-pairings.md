# Typography Pairings — Font Direction & Character Reference

This reference provides font personality descriptions, pairing strategies, and era-specific
type direction for logo design. Since we're generating via Gemini (not selecting actual fonts),
the goal is to describe the TYPE CHARACTER precisely so Gemini renders it correctly.

---

## Core Principle

You don't name fonts ("use Helvetica") — you describe their CHARACTER:
- Weight and proportions
- Stroke contrast (thick/thin variation)
- Terminal style (how strokes end)
- Letter-spacing and rhythm
- Mood and era

---

## The 5 Type Categories

### 1. Geometric Sans-Serif
**Character:** Circular O, uniform stroke weight, wide apertures, minimal humanist warmth.
Feels modern, clean, systematic.

**Describe as:**
- "Geometric sans-serif, circular letterforms, uniform stroke width"
- "Clean, minimal, based on perfect circles and straight lines"
- "Futura-like character — geometric, wide apertures, no stroke contrast"

**Best for:** Tech, startups, modern corporate, abstract marks

**Letter-spacing:** Medium to wide (0.05em – 0.15em)

**Weight range:** Light (200-300) for elegance, Bold (700-800) for impact

---

### 2. Humanist Sans-Serif
**Character:** Slightly oval O, subtle stroke variation, open counters, warm and approachable.
Feels human, friendly, professional without being cold.

**Describe as:**
- "Humanist sans-serif, slightly oval letterforms, subtle stroke variation"
- "Gill Sans / Optima character — warm, approachable, professional"
- "Open apertures, friendly proportions, not geometric"

**Best for:** Healthcare, education, consulting, B2B services

**Letter-spacing:** Normal to slightly wide (0.02em – 0.08em)

**Weight range:** Regular (400) to Semibold (600)

---

### 3. High-Contrast Serif
**Character:** Dramatic thick/thin stroke variation, vertical stress, fine hairlines, elegant.
Feels luxurious, editorial, timeless.

**Describe as:**
- "High-contrast serif, dramatic thick/thin strokes, Didot / Bodoni character"
- "Elegant, vertical stress, fine hairline serifs, luxurious"
- "Fashion-editorial quality, extreme contrast between thick and thin"

**Best for:** Luxury, fashion, fine dining, premium real estate

**Letter-spacing:** Wide (0.1em – 0.2em) for drama

**Weight range:** Light (300) to Regular (400) — heavy weights lose the contrast

---

### 4. Classic Serif
**Character:** Moderate stroke contrast, bracketed serifs, traditional proportions, readable.
Feels established, trustworthy, academic.

**Describe as:**
- "Classic serif, Garamond / Caslon / Baskerville character"
- "Moderate stroke contrast, bracketed serifs, traditional book-text proportions"
- "Timeless, refined, not dramatic — quiet authority"

**Best for:** Law, finance, universities, heritage brands

**Letter-spacing:** Normal to slightly wide (0.03em – 0.1em)

**Weight range:** Regular (400) to Medium (500)

---

### 5. Slab Serif
**Character:** Thick, blocky serifs, minimal stroke contrast, bold and sturdy.
Feels strong, American, vintage-friendly.

**Describe as:**
- "Slab serif, thick blocky serifs, minimal stroke contrast"
- "Rockwell / Clarendon character — bold, sturdy, confident"
- "Heavy serifs, strong presence, vintage Americana feel"

**Best for:** Vintage, craft, outdoor, sports, construction

**Letter-spacing:** Normal to tight (0em – 0.05em)

**Weight range:** Medium (500) to Bold (700)

---

## Script & Display Styles (Use Sparingly)

### Script (Handwritten / Calligraphic)
**Character:** Connected or flowing letterforms, organic, personal.

**Describe as:**
- "Flowing script, connected letterforms, confident and fluid — not casual"
- "Elegant calligraphy, upright posture, readable at small sizes"
- "Handwritten style with structure — not messy or overly decorative"

**Best for:** Luxury goods, weddings, artisan food, personal brands

**Avoid:** Overuse. Script works best as a secondary element or in Vintage/Classic styles.

---

### Display / Decorative
**Character:** Unique, stylized, often era-specific. Not meant for body text.

**Describe as:**
- "Art Deco display font, geometric, inline details, 1920s character"
- "Bold Western slab, extended width, decorative serifs"
- "Retro 1970s display, rounded terminals, groovy rhythm"

**Best for:** Vintage, entertainment, niche brands with strong personality

**Avoid:** Using display fonts without era/mood context — always anchor to a specific time or style.

## Pairing Strategies

### Strategy 1: Contrast (Serif + Sans)
Pair a serif wordmark with a sans-serif tagline, or vice versa.

**Example:**
- Wordmark: High-contrast serif (luxury)
- Tagline: Geometric sans, light weight, wide spacing (modern clarity)

**Why it works:** Visual hierarchy through typographic contrast.

---

### Strategy 2: Weight Contrast (Same Family)
Use the same type style but vary weight dramatically.

**Example:**
- Wordmark: Geometric sans, ExtraBold (800)
- Tagline: Same geometric sans, Light (200), increased letter-spacing

**Why it works:** Unity through family, hierarchy through weight.

---

### Strategy 3: Monotype (One Font, One Weight)
The entire logo uses a single typeface at a single weight. Relies on letter-spacing,
size, and layout for hierarchy.

**Example:**
- Wordmark: Classic serif, Regular (400), 0.15em letter-spacing
- Tagline: Same font, same weight, 0.25em letter-spacing, 40% smaller size

**Why it works:** Timeless, minimal, lets the name carry everything.

**Best for:** Classic, Corporate, Wordmark styles.

---

## Era-Specific Type Direction

### 1920s Art Deco
- Geometric display fonts with inline details
- High contrast, vertical emphasis
- Ornamental caps, restrained lowercase
- **Describe as:** "Art Deco display, geometric, inline strokes, 1920s elegance"

### 1950s Americana
- Bold slab serifs or rounded sans
- Script for secondary elements
- Wide, confident letter-spacing
- **Describe as:** "1950s slab serif, bold, Americana confidence, diner signage feel"

### 1970s Retro
- Rounded sans-serif, groovy rhythm
- Thick, bubbly letterforms
- Warm, playful, slightly psychedelic
- **Describe as:** "1970s rounded sans, thick bubbly strokes, retro groovy feel"

### 1890s Victorian
- Ornate serifs, decorative flourishes
- Hand-lettered quality, irregular baseline
- Dense, intricate
- **Describe as:** "Victorian ornate serif, decorative flourishes, hand-lettered 1890s character"

## Letter-Spacing Guidelines

| Style | Spacing | Why |
|-------|---------|-----|
| Classic serif wordmark | 0.1em – 0.2em | Elegance, breathing room |
| Geometric sans, all caps | 0.05em – 0.15em | Modern clarity |
| Script | 0em (connected) | Natural flow |
| Slab serif, bold | 0em – 0.05em | Sturdy, grounded |
| Tagline (any style) | +0.05em more than wordmark | Visual hierarchy |

## Custom Letterform Modifications

When requesting custom type treatments, be specific:

**Examples:**
- "The crossbar of the A is replaced with a thin horizontal line at 45°"
- "The terminal of the S is cut at a sharp angle, suggesting forward motion"
- "The dot of the i is replaced with a small geometric star"
- "The tail of the Q extends horizontally and becomes an arrow"

These modifications work best with Wordmark, Abstract, and Corporate styles.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Describing fonts by name alone** — "Use Helvetica" doesn't help Gemini. Describe the character.

❌ **Mixing too many type styles** — Two type families maximum. Three is chaos.

❌ **Script + script pairing** — Never pair two scripts. One script + one sans/serif.

❌ **Tight spacing on script** — Scripts need natural flow; forced tight spacing breaks rhythm.

❌ **Heavy weight + high contrast serif** — Heavy weights (700+) lose the elegant thin strokes.

---

## Final Checklist

Before finalizing type direction:

- [ ] Type character described precisely (not just "modern font")
- [ ] Letter-spacing specified (0.05em, 0.15em, etc.)
- [ ] Weight specified (Light 300, Bold 700, etc.)
- [ ] Caps vs. title case decided
- [ ] If pairing: contrast strategy clear (serif + sans, weight contrast, or monotype)
- [ ] Era-appropriate if Vintage style
- [ ] Custom modifications (if any) described anatomically
