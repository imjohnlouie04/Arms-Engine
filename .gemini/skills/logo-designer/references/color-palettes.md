## Usage Guidelines

**Two-color logos (most common):**
- Primary + White/Black
- Primary + Accent

**Three-color logos (use sparingly):**
- Primary + Secondary + Neutral (white/black/gray)
- Limit to Emblem, Vintage, or Mascot styles

**Monochrome (Classic, Corporate):**
- Single color + white space
- Relies entirely on form, not color

**Gradients (rarely):**
- Only for tech/startup brands explicitly requesting modern gradients
- Always provide a flat fallback version

---

## Testing Colors

Before finalizing, mentally test:

1. **One-color test:** Does it work in pure black? If not, the design is too color-dependent.
2. **Reverse test:** Does it work white-on-dark and dark-on-light?
3. **Small-scale test:** Are colors distinguishable at 32px?
4. **Industry fit:** Does the palette feel native to the industry, or borrowed from another?

---

## When to Break the Rules

Use unexpected color when:
- Client explicitly requests differentiation from competitors
- Brand personality is intentionally contrarian (e.g., a law firm that wants to feel approachable)
- The brand name or story has a strong color association (e.g., "Blue Ocean Consulting")

Always explain WHY an unconventional palette was chosen.
